\subsection{Spatial Summation and Classical Receptive Field Characterization}
To establish a baseline for neuronal activity, we first characterized the spatial summation properties of the V1 laminar microcircuit. By presenting centered visual stimuli of increasing diameters, we observed the typical transition from facilitation to suppression in excitatory populations. The classical receptive field (CRF) size was operationally defined as the stimulus diameter that evoked the maximum firing rate before the onset of surround modulation. In our baseline V1-only configuration, neurons exhibited a characteristic summation curve where the firing rate increased monotonically until reaching a plateau, followed by a modest reduction in activity as the stimulus expanded into the extra-classical regions.

\subsection{Comparison of Architectural Configurations for Surround Suppression}
We systematically evaluated the contribution of different connectivity patterns to the emergence of surround suppression. In the first configuration (V1 lateral connections only), the network produced a maximum suppression index of approximately 30--35\%, failing to reach the levels observed in physiological recordings. The second configuration, which incorporated non-specific V2 feedback without internal competitive dynamics, resulted in an overall increase in V1 excitability but did not significantly enhance the depth of suppression, often leading to linear gain modulation rather than non-linear contextual integration.

\subsection{Robust Suppression via Hierarchical Competitive Dynamics}
The integration of hierarchical competition within the V2 module (Configuration 3) fundamentally altered the network's response to large-scale stimuli. By enforcing lateral competition between V2 columns, the feedback signals became highly selective and contrast-dependent. This architecture achieved a robust surround suppression of approximately 75\%, closely matching experimental data from primate V1. Our results indicate that the non-linear refinement of top-down signals through inter-areal competition is the critical mechanism for reproducing high-magnitude extra-classical effects.

\subsection{Gamma-band Oscillations and E/I Balance}
Finally, we analyzed the temporal dynamics of the network during contextual modulation. The emergence of robust surround suppression was accompanied by strong oscillatory activity in the gamma-band range (30--60 Hz). Spectral analysis revealed that the hierarchical competitive framework promotes a precise excitation/inhibition (E/I) balance that sustains these oscillations. Furthermore, by systematically perturbing this balance to simulate pathological endophenotypes, we observed a significant reduction in both suppression depth and gamma power, providing a direct link between circuit-level dynamics and the neurophysiological markers associated with neuropsychiatric disorders.
