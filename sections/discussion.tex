\subsection{Emergence of eCRF Effects through Hierarchical Competition}
The present study demonstrates that robust extra-classical receptive field (eCRF) effects, particularly surround suppression, cannot be fully accounted for by local horizontal connections within V1 or by non-specific linear feedback. Our large-scale spiking network model reveals that a suppression magnitude of approximately 75\%—consistent with in vivo electrophysiological recordings—is only achieved when feedback signals are non-linearly refined by lateral competition within higher-order visual areas, such as V2. These findings challenge the traditional view of visual processing as a strictly local feature-extraction pipeline, suggesting instead that profound contextual modulation is an emergent property of inter-areal competitive dynamics. By integrating a biologically plausible laminar microcircuit with this hierarchical competitive framework, our model bridges the gap between anatomical constraints and the complex dynamics of global sensory integration.

\subsection{Computational Advantages of Non-linear Feedback}
From a computational perspective, embedding lateral competition within the feedback loop provides a highly efficient mechanism for predictive coding and sensory filtering. Simple additive or multiplicative top-down modulation often leads to runaway excitation or generalized gain changes that lack stimulus specificity. In contrast, our results indicate that inter-areal competition acts as a dynamic spatial filter, ensuring that feedback to V1 is tightly restricted to relevant contextual features. This architecture prevents the saturation of primary sensory areas and optimizes information transmission by suppressing redundant neural activity when a stimulus extends beyond the classical receptive field.

\subsection{Implications for Computational Psychiatry}
Beyond sensory processing, the mechanistic framework proposed here offers significant insights into the pathophysiology of neuropsychiatric disorders. The delicate balance between excitation and inhibition (E/I balance) required to sustain hierarchical competition is also critical for generating gamma-band oscillations. Our simulations show that perturbing this E/I balance directly impairs both the depth of surround suppression and the power of gamma oscillations, closely mirroring the sensory and oscillatory deficits frequently observed in patients with schizophrenia. Consequently, our spiking network model serves not only as a tool for understanding basic visual neuroscience but also as a translational platform in computational psychiatry, providing a circuit-level explanation for macroscopic clinical endophenotypes.

\subsection{Limitations and Future Directions}
While our model successfully captures the core dynamics of eCRF modulation, several avenues remain for future investigation. The current implementation relies on simplified point-neuron models (LIF) and generalized inhibitory pools. Future iterations will benefit from incorporating morphologically detailed neurons and distinguishing between specific interneuron subtypes (e.g., Parvalbumin and Somatostatin-expressing cells), which play distinct roles in lateral and feedback inhibition. Additionally, exploring how these network dynamics adapt under varying energetic constraints or metabolic states could further elucidate the biological limits of hierarchical sensory processing.
